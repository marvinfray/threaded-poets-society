---
title: The Church That Forgot Its Name
date: 2026-03-14
tags: [light, memory, ruin]
draft: true
commit_message: |
  This poem grew from a place that is very important in my life: Cwm-yr-Eglwys, a small valley on the Pembrokeshire coast whose name means "the valley of the church." At its centre stand the stone remains of a chapel, roofless now, open to the sky and the sea.

  I first visited there shortly after my mother died. She had suffered a fatal stroke on the day of my sister's wedding — after witnessing the church ceremony but before the celebration that was meant to follow. In the days that followed, my family gathered together in Cwm-yr-Eglwys: my father, my sisters, my wife and our children, my closest friend and his family, and my wife's mother. It was a time marked deeply by grief, yet something about that place — the openness of the valley, the presence and personality of the sea, the worn stone of the ruined church — held us.

  Looking back, I was surprised at how the memory carries an unexpected tenderness. In the midst of loss, the time and space we shared together was beautiful. Since then, we have returned to Cwm again and again. My brother-in-law's family has a place along that same stretch of coast, and the valley has become part of the rhythm of our lives.

  The image of that roofless church also echoes another place closer to home: the remains of St Thomas's Church ruins, whose surviving walls now hold the Southsea Rose Garden. There too, the roof is gone and the sky has taken its place. The walls remain, but the space within them has changed — quiet, open, alive in a different way.

  During meditation one day, the image of these places returned to me. I found myself thinking about the endurance of stone walls, the rising and setting of the sun and moon, and the quiet passage of time through spaces left open to the sky. It began to feel as though we ourselves are something like windows — life passing through us for a while. As we live, we become stained with experience, marked by love and loss, sometimes cracked by time and grief.

  Yet those marks are not what diminish us. They are what give us colour. They are what allow light to pass through us in ways that are uniquely our own. The imperfections, the fractures, the stories we carry — these are what make the light beautiful.
footnote: "thirty-two movements through a ruined church; what light does when it passes through old glass into older stone — what is held, what is translated, and what the crack in the north window reveals only by moonlight"

---

*i.*

The church has forgotten its name.

What it remembers: the east wall,
the west wall,
and between them, a rectangle of sky
the colour of whatever weather is passing through.

Stone. Rough-coursed. The mortar gone soft
in places, returning to the cliff it came from.
Grass inside the nave —
not unkempt, not kept.
Just grass, doing what grass does
when the roof has been absent long enough.

---

*ii.*

The sea is close but not visible from here.
It makes itself known by sound:
a low persistence, the kind of sound
that stops you noticing it
about thirty seconds after you arrive.

The graves outside lean
in their separate directions,
their names reduced to texture —
a ridge here, a hollow there,
the letters blurred to braille
that no one reads.

---

*iii.*

Two windows.

One in the north wall. One in the south.
Between them, the width of the church —
perhaps twelve feet. Perhaps fourteen.
The kind of measurement that varies
depending on where you stand to take it.

Each window holds its glass.
Coloured glass. Not much of it —
panels of amber, a deep blue,
a green that in certain lights
looks almost black.
The leading dark with age,
the glass itself pocked, uneven,
thick in some places, thin in others,
the way old things are uneven
without apology.

---

*iv.*

Morning light comes from the east.
It enters through the open chancel —
there is no east window now,
just the gap where the wall was taken
(by weather, not by hands) —
and it lays itself across the grass floor
in a long pale stripe
that shortens as the hours move.

The south window receives it first.

---

*v.*

To receive light is not to hold it.

The south window does something to the morning:
thickens the amber into something
you could almost put your hand through,
slows the blue to the kind of blue
you find in deep water,
at depth, where the cold begins.

The light that passes through
lands on the north wall.

Not the window. The wall itself —
the stone beside and below and above
the north window —
washed in the colours
the south glass has made of the morning.

---

*vi.*

Here is a thing the church has always known:

that what arrives at one wall
has come through the other.

That stone receives what glass transforms.

That the north wall holds, for a little while,
the memory of the south window's colour —
amber and blue and almost-black-green
moving slowly across the stone
as the morning moves,
as the season tilts the angle of arrival,
as the cloud comes and goes
without announcement.

---

*vii.*

There are birds that use the walls.

A wren has a run in the west wall's rubble —
three or four courses up,
a gap in the stone just wide enough
for a wren to be what a wren is
when it is not being watched.

In the mornings, before the light shifts south,
it sings from the top of the east gap.
Not performing. Not projecting.
Just making the sound that completes
whatever it is a wren completes
when it sings from high ground
into a roofless space.

---

*viii.*

Autumn.

The angle of the sun changes
the way a body changes
when it has carried something —
slightly, and then
all at once.

The afternoon light now enters
from the south-west,
and the south window
burns longer, later,
holds its amber into the blue hour
past when you'd expect it.

The north wall receives this.

The stone darkens into colour —
a warmth it does not naturally own,
borrowed from the south,
held for the duration of an afternoon,
released when the light fails.

---

*ix.*

What the north window does with moonlight
is a different matter.

There is no performing in it.
The moon does not choose the north window
the way the sun chooses the south.
It simply happens that the moon,
in its particular orbit,
passes behind the north-facing glass
in the small hours —
and the colours that result
are not the same colours.

The blue is not the deep-water blue.
It is the blue of something that has waited.

The amber is not warmth.
It is the colour of preserved things —
resin, honey, wings caught mid-flight
and held.

---

*x.*

Summer, and the grass inside the nave
grows long enough to move.

The south window in the afternoon
casts its colours all the way across the floor —
amber and blue and the green
that is almost black —
and they move across the grass
the way a hand moves
across a surface it is learning.

Slow. Without hurry.
Attending to the uneven.

---

*xi.*

There is no door now.

The west end is open to the path,
the path to the lane,
the lane to the village
that keeps its distance from this place —
not from fear, not from forgetting,
but the way a village learns
to let some things remain
at the edge of its knowing,
present but unclaimed.

---

*xii.*

The south window.

Look at it straight on — which is to say,
stand in the north-facing wall's place,
which you cannot do
without standing inside the church,
in the grass where the nave was,
looking south —

and the colours arrange themselves
into a figure.
Not a person. Not yet.
A figure in the loose sense:
something that coheres, that has
a logic of adjacency,
that the eye, given enough light,
given enough patience,
reads as form.

---

*xiii.*

The north window is older.
You can tell by the leading —
the lines of it less regular,
the came flattened in places
as if the weight of glass
has been pressing down
for longer than the south window's glass
has been pressing.

The north window holds its colours
differently:
not radiant, but present.
The way a face holds
what it has come through —
not as wound, not as story,
but as the particular patience
of features that have been
used for a long time.

---

*xiv.*

Spring, and the light returns to an angle
the stone has not forgotten.

The south window catches the late morning —
ten, eleven o'clock —
and for an hour
the floor of the nave is full of colour.

The north wall warms.
Not from within. From without.
From the south.

The stone receives it
the way stone receives everything:
without comment,
but not without change.

---

*xv.*

What passes between them
is not what either contains.

The south window's amber
is not the amber on the north wall —
it has been translated,
altered by the twelve or fourteen feet
of air between,
by the particles the air holds,
by the angle of incidence,
by the dust.

What arrives is a version.
What is held is a version of a version.
And what the north wall holds
when the light moves on
is an afterimage —
not the colour, but the memory of the colour,
which is a different thing
and perhaps more truthful.

---

*xvi.*

Winter.

The grass flattens to brown and grey.
The walls darken with rain.
The sky lowers itself to just above the east gap
and stays there.

The south window goes dim.

Not black — the glass is still glass,
still coloured, still arranged
in its figure that the eye reads as form —
but the light behind it
has gone thin, has gone far away,
and what comes through
is barely enough to call colour.

The north wall receives a grey
with a memory of amber in it.
Less than warmth. More than nothing.

---

*xvii.*

On a night in February,
when the moon is near full
and the cloud thins to gauze,
the moonlight enters the north-facing window.

And here —

under this light, this borrowed light,
this light that is the sun's light
given back from a distance,
altered by passage, by reflection,
by the cold —

here, in the glass of the north window,
there is a crack.

---

*xviii.*

It runs from the lower left
to just past the centre.

Not a break. Not a fracture.
A line. A resolved line.

The kind of line a thing carries
after it has held something
for a long time,
after the holding has become
so habitual
that the carrying and the carried
are no longer distinguishable —

and then there is a cold night,
or a shift in the stone,
or simply the fullness of time —
and the glass,
which has been one thing,
becomes one thing
with a line in it.

---

*xix.*

Under the sun, the crack is invisible.

The south window's projection is so vivid,
the amber so insistent,
the blue so full of its own depth,
that the north wall takes the whole
without reservation —

and the crack in the north window,
backlit by the strong light,
reads as shadow only,
part of the leading's pattern,
unremarkable,
like a fine hair on a familiar hand
that you have never thought to mention.

---

*xx.*

But the moon shows what the sun obscures.

Moonlight is patient.
It does not insist.
It moves slowly across the glass
and where the glass is whole
it passes through
and makes its cool translation —
and where the glass is cracked
the moonlight enters differently,
refracts at a new angle,
scatters into a thin bright line
on the opposite wall —
on the south wall,
on the place where the south window sits —

and what the south window receives
is the north window's crack,
thrown in light,
precise,
unasked for,
true.

---

*xxi.*

The church has forgotten its name.

But the walls have not forgotten each other.

The distance between them —
twelve feet, or fourteen,
depending on where you stand —
is full of what has passed between them:
light transformed, light returned,
the amber held and released,
the blue deepened and thinned,
the crack's slow making,
the crack's slow revelation.

---

*xxii.*

The wren has come back.

It sings from the east gap in the morning
and the song moves through the roofless space
and is not lost.

The grass in the nave grows.
The graves outside lean.
The lane leads to the village
that keeps its distance
and calls it respect.

---

*xxiii.*

There are days in April —
low cloud, quick sun, quick cloud —
when the light through the south window
moves so fast across the floor
that the colours seem to breathe,
seem to enter and withdraw
in something like a rhythm —

and the north wall receives this
and gives it back
in the particular stillness of stone —

and the interval between them
is neither empty nor full
but charged,
the way air is charged
in the moment before rain
makes good on what it promised.

---

*xxiv.*

What the crack holds is not damage.

It holds the particular angle
at which a long stress finally
made itself visible —
not catastrophe,
but the record of endurance,
the line that says:
here is where I bore the weight,
here is where the cold entered deepest,
here is where the bearing
left its mark on the bearing.

Under moonlight, it is luminous.
Under sunlight, it is nothing.

Both are true.
Neither is the whole truth.

---

*xxv.*

Late autumn now, and the light
from the south window
comes at an angle so low
that it enters the north window
not just the north wall —
enters the glass itself,
illuminates the colours from behind,
the amber from behind the amber,
the blue made suddenly interior —

and the south window's light
reads the north window's colours
back to itself,
finds in them something
it did not know it was projecting —

a blue it recognises
as its own blue, deepened —
an amber that is its amber
aged, made patient —

the crack, which the sunlight
cannot see, which the sunlight
has never seen —

---

*xxvi.*

The sea is still close.

In the night, when the wind comes north,
you can hear it more clearly —
its low persistence
working at the base of the cliff,
which is the base of the stone,
which is the base of the walls,
which have stood here
longer than their purpose
and will stand longer than their standing.

---

*xxvii.*

What the glass does is not contain.

It holds colour the way a word holds meaning —
which is to say, conditionally,
dependent on the light behind it,
the eye in front of it,
the distance between
the word and the one who reads it.

What passes through is real.
What changes in the passing is also real.
The change is not corruption.

---

*xxviii.*

Midwinter.

The south window is dim.
The north window is dim.
The sky between the walls
is the colour of used pewter —
not bleak, not beautiful,
simply the colour things are
when the light has contracted
to its most honest quantity.

The stone holds nothing
it was given this morning.
The stone holds only stone.

And yet —

the crack is there.
And the geometry of the leading.
And the colours, waiting.
And the interval of twelve or fourteen feet,
which is not nothing,
which has never been nothing,
which the light will cross again
when the light comes back.

---

*xxix.*

The church has forgotten its name.

The grass. The walls. The sky.
The graves that lean.
The lane. The village at its distance.
The wren in its gap in the west wall's rubble.

Two windows.
The interval between them.
What has passed through.
What remains.

---

*xxx.*

One morning —
a morning in late March,
the sun finally at the angle
where it clears the east gap
and enters straight and strong,
the air still cold but the light
already warmth, already arrival —

the south window catches it
full on,
and burns.

Amber. Blue. The green
that in certain lights looks almost black.

And the north wall receives it —
all of it, all at once —
and for the length of an hour
the stone is not stone
but the temporary home of colour,
the place where what has been sent
is held,
before it moves on,
before the angle shifts,
before the light remembers
it has other walls to visit.

---

*xxxi.*

The crack, under this sun, is invisible.

But the wall knows it is there.
The light, passing through,
has always passed through it —
has always refracted there
without knowing what it was refracting —
has always made its landing
on the north wall
slightly altered by that fine division —

and the north wall
has always received it —
the version, the version of the version,
the light that has been through
more than it knows —

and held it —

and given it back as warmth —

as warmth, and nothing more complicated than warmth —

---

*xxxii.*

The church has forgotten its name.

What it remembers: the east wall.
The west wall.
And between them — not emptiness.

Never emptiness.

The interval.
The charged and patient interval.
The crossing and the crossed.
The amber held in stone for an hour
and released.
The crack that moonlight writes in light
on the opposite wall.
The wren's song entering the roofless space
and not being lost.

The sea.
The sea.
The low persistence of the sea.
