---
title: Bearing Witness
date: 2026-04-01
tags: [fatherhood, love, grief, witness]
---

I remember fragments of the nights.
Enough to know they happened.
Enough to know the body
was asked to do things
the mind has since filed away —
protection, not ingratitude.

But I remember the mornings.  
God, I remember the mornings —  
small hands finding mine  
before their eyes were open,  
the world not yet a thing  
they had to meet alone.  

Daddy.  
Like a door opening.  
Like something sacred  
said without knowing  
it was sacred.  

I was everything, then.  
Every question answered.  
Every monster named and dismissed.  
I held the whole cartography  
of what was safe,  
and they trusted it —  
they trusted me —  
with a completeness  
I have not known since.  

We were supposed to be getting somewhere.  
I see that now.  
The sleepless nights I've mercifully forgotten,  
the tantrums I recall  
only as weather —  
all of it was passage,  
not arrival.  
The sticky hands were learning  
to let go.  
The falling was rehearsal.  
Even the love —  
even the love was practice  
for a love they'd take  
somewhere I couldn't follow.  

They are grown now —  
or growing into it —  
into shapes  
I recognise  
but didn’t design.  

I used to be responsible for them.  
Every breath a debt  
I owed the world.  
Now I am responsible to them —  
which is quieter,  
and harder,  
and nothing like I expected.  

It becomes more bearing witness  
than stepping in.  
I turned that over like a stone,  
looking for what lived underneath.  

Bearing witness.  
Standing at the edge of a life  
that belongs to someone else now —  
someone who used to belong to me,  
or so I thought.  

There is a grief in this  
that has no clean name.  
Not loss, exactly.  
Not pride, exactly.  
Something in between,  
something that sits in the chest  
on a Sunday afternoon  
when the house is too quiet  
and you find yourself listening  
for a voice  
that used to call you Daddy.  

But here is what I'm learning —  
slowly,  
imperfectly,  
with all the grace of a man  
still working it out:  

To witness is not to withdraw.  
It is to see.  
Fully.  
Without the urgency to fix,  
without the need to be needed.  

To stand at the edge and say: I am here.  
Not to catch you —  
you don’t need catching —  
but to watch  
the extraordinary thing  
you’ve become.  

To know that the nights I can't remember  
made the mornings they carry with them.  
That somewhere in my forgetting  
is the evidence  
of everything I gave.  

I miss being Daddy.  
I won't pretend otherwise.  
But I am learning  
a different kind of love —  
the kind that doesn't hold,  
the kind that doesn't need  
to be the answer.  

Just present.  
Just watching.  
Just here.  

Bearing witness  
to the best thing  
I ever had a hand in.
