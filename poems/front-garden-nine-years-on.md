I wrote you down the night before —  
that you saw the good in us  
when we couldn't, that you spent your grace  
more freely on us than we'd spend it on ourselves.  

Then morning. The front garden  
full of wings. Not descended,  
the way the Sky God has it,  
but risen — up from the vegetable bed,  

from the leaves we grew and they ate first.  
The caterpillars took their fill.  
Hunger before flight; the soil  
holding up its end. Nothing lifts  

that hasn't fed down low.  
And here is the thing I keep turning:  
I'm the age now to be the watcher,  
to wince at the hard viewing and not look away,  

to see my own flaws walking round  
in the ones I love. You stood here once.  
You leave and return on the same wing —  
the going and the coming back, one motion.  

So I'll try. To give it inward, I mean,  
the grace. To read the small green hungers  
for what they are — not ruin, but the cocoon  
getting made. The flight already deciding to begin.
