commit_message: |
  This poem grew from a conversation about language, fundamentalism, and
  the conditions needed for genuine discourse. The question underneath all
  of it: how do you institutionalise wondering without the institution
  gradually replacing the wondering?

  The lens I think we should look through is one of elderhood, childhood, 
  and adolescence as complementary rather than hierarchical. Each holds a 
  weight the others cannot carry. The union is not in levelling that weight 
  but in learning to bear it together.

  I wonder if to respect adolescence is the hardest ask,
  and perhaps the most necessary one.

The elder does not speak to fill the room —  
she speaks because the room has earned her silence,  
and broken it.  
To hear her is to hold  
what time has weighed and kept.  

The child does not wonder as a rehearsal —  
she wonders because the world  
has not yet told her not to.  
To see through her eyes  
is to unlearn the useful blindness  
we mistook for wisdom.  

And the adolescent —  
restless, uncontained —  
do not mistake that heat for error.  
That is the friction that prevents  
the stone from settling  
into its own grave.  

None of these is incomplete.  
None is a draft of the others.  
Each holds a weight  
the others cannot carry —  
and the union is not  
in the levelling of that weight,  
but in the learning  
to bear it together.  

This is what respect asks of us:  
not agreement,  
not deference,  
not the performance of listening —  
but genuine curiosity  
about what it is  
to inhabit a life  
not your own.  

To wonder together  
is the institution.  
The discipline is showing up  
to not-know —  
in compliment.
