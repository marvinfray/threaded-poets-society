  This piece came from a meditation on interruption, control, and the subtle instinct to withdraw in moments of intensity. 
  Cold water, held breath — these practices helped me recognise the "flinch" — the body's early signal to retreat — and to sit with it long enough for the experience to change.
  In reflecting on intimacy, I began to see the same pattern at play. 
  Moments of closeness can carry an undercurrent of self-doubt or hesitation, often leading to a quiet pulling away. 
  What this poem explores is the possibility that intimacy is not the absence of these interruptions, but the willingness to remain, and to return, when they arise.
  In this sense, trust is not only built on safety, but on a shared confidence that even when the moment breaks, it can be found again.

It begins before touch.  
A thought — small, almost polite — arrives uninvited:  
Are you enough for this?  
And the body listens.  
Not to what is real, but to what might be — a tightening, a quiet withdrawal, the flinch before contact.  

I have known this place.  
Where closeness feels like exposure, and exposure feels like danger, though nothing in the room has moved against me.  
Just memory, just story, just a learned hesitation wearing the shape of truth.  

But I have been practising something.  
Not here, not in this space between us — but under cold water, in held breath, in moments where the body says no before it understands.  
There too, the same voice:  
Step back. End this. Return to comfort.  
And there too, a choice —  
not to silence it, but to stay.  

Long enough for the sharp edge to soften, for the panic to pass through, for sensation to unfold into something else.  
Not ease, not quite —  
but presence.  

So now, here —  
with you —  
when the thought arrives, when the moment wavers, when something in me reaches for retreat,  
I recognise it.  
Not as truth, but as habit.  
And I stay.  

Not perfectly. Not always without movement.  
But longer than before.  
Long enough to feel what is actually here:  
warmth, breath, the quiet weight of trust being formed in real time.  

Because intimacy is not the absence of fear.  
It is not a perfect stillness held without interruption.  
It is this —  
the willingness to remain when something in you says leave,  
the courage to be seen while still unsure,  
the soft, repeated act of returning to the moment we almost lost.  

And maybe that is trust.  
Not that nothing will break — but that when it does,  
we will not disappear.  
We will find our way back to each other,  
and begin again from there.
