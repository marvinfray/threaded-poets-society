footnote: "The poem begins in a dream and ends in one. The bells don't change. Everything else does — until it doesn't have to."
commit_message: 
  A poem about sound as the container of love — and the impulse, repeated across a lifetime, to hand the headphones over. Waking as a child into the bells of St Mary's. Carrying my mother back through those same doors. Thirty thousand feet and a stranger beside me, another plane alongside ours, Jeff Mills in my ears — The Bells — passed across without a word and handed back with four. My stag do, the Midlands mix, friends who know the untranslatable parts of me.
---

I used to wake into the bells —  
St Mary's, end of our road, arriving through the window  
before I was fully back in myself,  
still loose in whatever the night had made,  
the summer already certain  
of things I hadn't caught up to yet.  

Planes cut clean lines through blue sky,  
gliding forward, certain,  
something to watch,  
something to measure yourself against  
without ever being inside it.  

The ice cream van turned the corner  
and Greensleeves did what it always did —  
made the ordinary feel like a gift  
I hadn't thought to ask for.  

The crackle of a needle finding its groove.  
A tape rewinding — backwards, then forwards again.  
Michael on the screen, moonwalking,  
gliding against the pull of things,  
moving back and somehow still arriving.  

I thought that's how it worked —  
that you could step against time,  
that rhythm might carry you clean through it.  

These are the sounds that made me.  
The ones I carry now like a hand  
pressed flat against the chest —  
*still here, still here, still here.*  

---

Then on that day I walked back into St Mary's.  
Forward.  

The bells were the same bells.  
The road was the same road.  
But I was carrying my mother.  

My Dad. My Uncle.  
My best friend. All of us  
stepping through a door  
into a world I hadn't been shown the map for.  

The bells rang.  
Unchanged.  
Everything else irrevocably different.  

---

Years later, thirty thousand feet,  
and I am inside the glide now.  
No longer looking up, but held in it —  
carried forward through something vast  
I do not control.  

A stranger beside me —  
a man of few words and good ones —  
when another plane appeared at our window.  
Just there. Alongside us.  
Two vessels in the same sky,  
briefly parallel, briefly everything.  

No forwards or backwards then —  
just a moment held,  
like breath at the edge of something.  

I was listening to Jeff Mills.  
*The Bells.*  
I took the headphones off  
and handed them over.  

He listened.  
He smiled.  
He handed them back.  

*Quite something, eh.*  

Four words. Exactly right.  
The understatement that holds the universe still.  

---

At my stag do, high and held  
by friends who know the untranslatable parts of me,  
Midland played and I came apart into patterns —  
ebb and flow, in and out of myself,  
as if I could move through the moment  
and watch it at the same time.  

A kind of gliding — not forward, not back, but through.  

A journey taken together  
in a room that no longer exists  
except in all of us.  

I hope they carry it the way I carry it.  
I hope when the music finds them —  
somewhere, years from now —  
they press a hand to their chest  
and feel it still moving.  

---

Sometimes I imagine my own funeral.  
Not with dread — with reverence.  
The ones I love, gathered.  
And I want to have been worth the gathering.  

I want to have handed the headphones over  
enough times.  
I want someone to turn to someone  
and say — without quite knowing why —  

*quite something, eh.*  

And mean me.  

And then — go the way I came.  
Back into the bells.  
Back into the dream.  
The summer already knowing.
