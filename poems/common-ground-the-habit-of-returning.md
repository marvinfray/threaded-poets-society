commit_message: |
  On the tension between sameness and difference — and the shared values that make the space between them worth inhabiting.
  Sameness gives us the capacity to connect; difference gives us the reason to; context tells us how; and shared values make it matter.
  The poem began as a philosophical question: what makes similarity and difference useful or valuable? The answer kept returning to context, and then to the agreements we make — quietly, habitually — that turn context into community.
  Arendt's plurality sits underneath this: the same ensures we can understand each other, the different ensures we need each other. Aristotle's phronesis too — virtue as contextually sensitive, not abstractly universal. And Ubuntu: personhood as irreducibly relational.
  The fence arrived as both image and warning. Every time we name what we share, we risk drawing a line around it. The poem tries to hold that tension without resolving it — which is why it ends warm but with someone, somewhere, always counting the hands.
  The field, the fire, the gate left on the latch — all ways of asking what common ground actually costs, and who tends it.

We are built the same underneath —  
the same dark pocket of hunger,  
the same small catastrophe of breath,  
the same moon pulling at the water in us.  
Same enough, at least, to read each other's silence,  
to know when a hand on a shoulder  
means *I am here*  
without the word.  

But you hold your grief at a different angle.  
Your joy arrives with a stranger's accent.  
The thing that breaks you open  
is not the thing that breaks me —  
and this gap, this necessary distance,  
is not a flaw in the design.  
It is the design.  
Different enough that I lean toward you  
the way a room leans toward a window.  

Between us: a table.  
A season. A word  
we use the same way  
for different reasons.  
The context that makes us  
more than the sum of our aloneness —  
a field where the light shifts  
depending on where you stand.  

What holds the field together  
is the habit of returning —  
the gate left on the latch,  
the cup left out for someone,  
the invitation to learn each other's names  
before we learn each other's stories  
and love both anyway.  
A fire tends itself when enough hands feed it —  
though someone, somewhere, is always counting the hands.  

This is the place worth inhabiting —  
not the sameness, not the difference,  
but the breath of space between,  
where shared values draw the walls gently  
and we discover, again,  
that understanding was always the beginning,  
that need was always the invitation,  
and that somewhere between  
what we have in common  
and what we will never share  
is every home we've ever loved.
