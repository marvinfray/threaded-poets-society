footnote: |
  This poem began with a reflection on colonialism, caste, race, and the stories societies tell about who we are.
  Stephen Jenkinson advises that, as an elder, rather than sitting across from someone and telling them what to think, we might consider "changing the seating arrangement" — sitting beside them and looking out at the same landscape together.
  The poem explores the inherited furniture of identity: the ideas, labels, stories and assumptions that arrive before we do. It is less interested in whether those inheritances are right or wrong than in the moment we notice them, and begin to wonder how they came to be there.
commit_message: |
  Written while reflecting on inherited narratives, conceptual furniture, and the difference between instruction and accompaniment.
  This poem does not seek to dismantle the house. It simply invites us to notice the fingerprints on the walls, pull up a chair, and look out together.
  Not to decide what is true.
  To wonder how we came to know.

Pull up a chair.  
Not across from me.  
Here.  
Beside me.  
Let us look out together.  

I'm not here to tell you who you are.  
Only to wonder how you came to know.  

Who handed you your name?  
Who taught you where to stand?  
Who pointed at the horizon and called it success?  
Who told you what a man is?  
What a woman is?  
What it means to belong?  

We inherit more than eye colour.  
More than the shape of a nose or the curl of a smile.  
We inherit stories.  
Ideas worn smooth by the hands of those who carried them before us.  
Some are gifts.  
Some are burdens.  
Most are both.  

A child arrives in a room already furnished.  
The chairs are set.  
The pictures are hung.  
The drawers are labelled.  
This is right.  
This is wrong.  
This is yours.  
This is theirs.  
And for a while it all seems natural.  
As though the furniture grew there.  
As though no one built the house.  
As though no one chose where to place the walls.  

Then one day,  
if we are fortunate,  
we notice the fingerprints.  

And look down at our hands.  
And wonder whose walls we have been building.
