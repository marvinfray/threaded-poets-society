What sustains the breath we take?  
The exhale of trees, the inhale of earth,  
Feeding the root, bearing the fruit, 
A cycle unbroken, turning anew.  

Does it begin in the seedling's hope,  
Or in the soil's labor?  
Perhaps in the rain's soft hands  
Or the sun's faithful, tireless arc.  
It moves in rhythms, unseen yet constant,  
Nourishing what was, what is, what will be.  

Yet we — standing in the forest —  
Forget to feel the breath beneath.  
We marvel at the canopy and curse the fall,  
But rarely bow to the patient root,  
The unseen network that holds us all.  
What sustains the root? What does it sustain?  

Is it the breath of those who came before,  
The whispered wisdom of their hands,  
Or the promise of air not yet tasted,  
Held in the gentle grasp of tomorrow?  
The tree stands because the root reaches —  
Downward, inward, finding its sustenance.  

And we, too, are held by unseen threads,  
Tied to the earth, to each other,  
To the breaths we borrow and the futures we exhale.  
What sustains us? What do we sustain?  
Each action, a breeze or a storm,  
Each word, a seed cast into the wind.  

The cycle asks not for perfection,  
Only for care —  
To breathe and be breathed,  
To bear and to be borne,  
To turn and to return.  
A harmony found in the sustaining,  
In the giving and the receiving,  
In feeding the root while bearing the fruit.
