  Written from a playlist built for the road — and a memory of India that ended with Led Zeppelin and a friend whose name means Written.
  The poem traces a muddled, borrowed, real kind of freedom: Saudi Arabia and the strange gift of racial ambiguity; Australia and its roof terraces; India and the friends made there for life. At its centre is Likhit — a name that felt like fate — and an ending that arrived like a song: carried like cargo, headphones in, eyes closing, waking at the airport as if the whole thing had been a dream.

I never hitched across continents  
or slept in a station with everything I owned  
on my back.  
My freedom came sideways —  
commissioned, ticketed, per diem'd —  
but it found me anyway,  
the way weather finds you  
even inside.  

In Saudi I lost my passport  
and with it, briefly, myself.  
Stranded in a place that held its shape  
like something carved from heat and prayer,  
people called to me in Arabic,  
assumed I belonged,  
and I stood in that assumption  
and felt the edges of my own face  
differently.  
Mixed, mixed, mixed —  
what a word for the thing I am.  
Here, I was simply one of them.  
It unsettled me. It settled something too.  

In Australia I tried to be poor and free  
but there was a roof terrace,  
a pool,  
and the backpackers kept finding me  
like flies to an open bottle of Corona.  
I fell briefly in love there —  
cleanly,  
the way you fall in love  
when the flight home is already booked.  

But India.  

India is where the friends were made  
who will carry your name into old age.  

In Goa we danced until the moon  
was all that was left of the sky,  
and whatever we'd taken made the stars seem  
like they were breathing,  
and Abi and Lissy were there  
before they weren't —  
before they turned their faces south  
and were swallowed whole by Southeast Asia  
and I was left with just the residue of the night,  
standing at the ocean's edge  
like the Fool at the cliff —  
pack gone, friends gone,  
only a dog appearing from somewhere  
to stand beside me  
and look out at the same water,  
unbothered,  
as if this was always the plan.  

In Rajasthan, on a rooftop,  
an Afghan man and I  
sat with the smoke between us like a treaty,  
comparing the countries we carried in our chests,  
while below and beyond and impossible,  
Beyoncé sang at a princess's wedding —  
her voice rising over the palace walls  
into our ordinary miracle of a night.  

This is the muddled gift of it.  
Not the postcard journey.  
Not the gap year mythology.  
Just the strange, lit, borrowed, real thing.  

Between those nights, there was a balcony  
in Lonavala —  
a hill station where the world  
arranged itself below us  
like something we'd been handed.  
We sat together, Abi and I,  
looking out over the green and the distance,  
the kind of quiet that earns its name.  
Except.  
Beneath us, a road —  
horns, engines, the whole insistence of it,  
a river of noise that didn't know  
and didn't care  
that we were up here  
trying to be still.  
And still.  
We were still.  
Her beside me was the stillness.  
The road was just the road.  

And at the centre of it: Likhit.  

Likhit (लिख्), which means *Written.*  

Of course it does.  
Of course the best friend I'd find  
at the far end of the world  
would be named for the thing I trust most —  
the word already placed,  
the sentence that was always coming.  

On the last night we didn't sleep.  
What would be the point?  
We drank instead,  
let the hours go soft around us  
the way they do  
when you've stopped fighting them.  

When the taxi came  
they picked me up — all of them, laughing —  
lifted me like a rolled carpet,  
and poured me into the back seat.  

I put my headphones on.  

[*Over the hills and far away.*](https://open.spotify.com/playlist/6tqfrfKNqsVi0MDtmw1yKY)  

Of course it was.  

I closed my eyes.  

I don't remember it finishing.  

I woke at the airport  
as if the whole thing had been a dream —  
not mine, exactly,  
but one I had wandered into  
and been welcome in,  

carried off  
by a friend whose name means Written,  
into a song that already knew  
I was gone.
